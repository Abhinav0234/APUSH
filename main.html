Unit 1 (1491–1607)

Before European contact, North America hosted diverse societies—Mississippian city-states like Cahokia, nomadic Plains tribes, and complex Iroquois confederacies—each adapting to their environment.

1620–1607 saw initial Spanish and French incursions. The Columbian Exchange introduced Old World diseases that decimated native populations, while new crops reshaped global agriculture.

  • Major cultures: Cahokia, Iroquois, Pueblo
  • Columbian Exchange: maize, potatoes, smallpox
  • Labor: encomienda → early African slavery

Key Terms: Cahokia, encomienda, mestizo

Unit 2 (1607–1754)

English colonies took root: Jamestown’s tobacco economy fueled settlement, while New England Puritans built tight-knit religious communities after 1620’s Plymouth landing.

Regional differences emerged—New England’s mixed agriculture vs. Chesapeake’s plantations. Indentured servitude gave way to racialized slavery by mid-1700s.

  • Jamestown (1607) & Plymouth (1620)
  • Mayflower Compact & House of Burgesses
  • Growth of slavery & Bacon’s Rebellion

Key Terms: Mayflower Compact, headright system, Bacon’s Rebellion

Unit 3 (1754–1800)

The French & Indian War’s costs led Britain to tax colonies (Stamp, Townshend Acts), sparking resistance: “no taxation without representation.”

Revolution erupted in 1775, Declaration in 1776, and alliance with France secured victory by 1783. The new nation replaced the Articles of Confederation with the Constitution (1787) and Bill of Rights (1791).

  • Stamp Act (1765) & Boston Tea Party (1773)
  • Declaration of Independence (1776)
  • Constitution & Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist debates

Key Terms: Stamp Act, Articles of Confederation, Bill of Rights

Unit 4 (1800–1848)

Jefferson’s Louisiana Purchase (1803) doubled U.S. lands. Marbury v. Madison (1803) asserted judicial review. The Market Revolution linked regions via canals, railroads, and telegraphs.

Social reform movements—abolition, temperance, women’s rights (Seneca Falls 1848)—crystallized as Americans debated slavery’s expansion into new territories.

  • Louisiana Purchase & Lewis & Clark expedition
  • Erie Canal & steamboat networks
  • Seneca Falls Convention & Dorothea Dix reforms

Key Terms: Manifest Destiny, Missouri Compromise

Unit 5 (1844–1877)

The 1850s saw violent clashes over slavery (Bleeding Kansas), the Dred Scott decision (1857), and John Brown’s raid. War broke out in 1861; the Emancipation Proclamation (1863) reframed conflict as moral crusade.

Union victory (1865) abolished slavery (13th Amendment). Reconstruction amended citizenship rights (14th, 15th), but white Southern backlash instituted Jim Crow by 1877.

  • Compromise of 1850 & Kansas-Nebraska Act
  • Civil War battles & Sherman’s March
  • Reconstruction Amendments & Black Codes

Key Terms: Emancipation Proclamation, Radical Republicans

Unit 6 (1865–1898)

The Gilded Age saw rapid industrialization—Carnegie’s steel, Rockefeller’s oil—alongside massive immigration fueling urban growth and political machines.

Labor unrest (Haymarket Riot 1886, Pullman Strike 1894) prompted calls for reform, while federal policies (Homestead, Dawes Acts) displaced Native Americans.

  • Rise of trusts & Social Darwinism
  • Knights of Labor & AFL formation
  • Tenements & Tammany Hall

Key Terms: Gospel of Wealth, political machines

Unit 7 (1890–1945)

American imperialism expanded: Spanish-American War (1898) gained overseas territories; Panama Canal (1914) linked oceans. Progressives attacked monopolies and championed women’s suffrage.

WWI ushered U.S. onto world stage; the Great Depression (1929) led to FDR’s New Deal. WWII mobilization transformed economy and society, culminating in D-Day (1944) and Allied victory.

  • Spanish-American War & Platt Amendment
  • 19th Amendment & muckrakers
  • CCC, WPA & atomic bombings

Key Terms: New Deal, Lend-Lease

Unit 8 (1945–1980)

The Cold War dominated: Truman Doctrine (1947), Marshall Plan, NATO (1949). Proxy conflicts in Korea and Vietnam tested containment, while McCarthyism stoked domestic fears.

1960s–70s saw Civil Rights achievements (CR Act ’64, Voting Rights ’65), counterculture revolts, women’s liberation, and the environmental movement (EPA ’70). Watergate led to Nixon’s resignation (1974).

  • Brown v. Board & Civil Rights Act
  • Korean & Vietnam Wars
  • Watergate & détente

Key Terms: McCarthyism, détente

Unit 9 (1980–Present)

Reaganomics and the fall of the Berlin Wall (1989) ended Cold War. 9/11 (2001) spurred the War on Terror. The 2008 financial crisis led to Dodd-Frank and ACA health reform.

In the 21st century, globalization, digital technology, and climate change debates shape American politics and society.

  • Reagan Revolution & "tear down this wall"
  • PATRIOT Act & Iraq/Afghanistan
  • Great Recession & Affordable Care Act

Key Terms: globalization, social media age

APUSH Exam Structure

The AP U.S. History exam (3 hr 15 min) is divided into four sections:

  • Section I: Multiple-Choice & Stimulus (55 min, 40%): 55 questions, including 10-12 with primary/secondary sources.
  • Section II A: Short Answer Questions (SAQs) (50 min, 20%): 3 prompts requiring evidence-based responses.
  • Section II B: Document-Based Question (DBQ) (60 min, 25%): Analyze 4–6 documents + a prompt.
  • Section II C: Long Essay Question (LEQ) (40 min, 15%): Choose 1 of 3 prompts to craft a thesis-driven essay.

Time management is critical: allot minutes to outline essays, cite documents in DBQ, and leave time for proofreading. A clear thesis and strong evidence are keys to a 5.
